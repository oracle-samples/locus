# 1. Dietary Iron and Absorption Mechanisms

## 1.1 Forms of Dietary Iron: Heme vs Non‑Heme

- **Non‑heme iron**
  - Predominant in plant foods and most supplements.
  - Ingested mainly as **ferric (Fe³⁺)** iron, which must be reduced to Fe²⁺ for uptake (doc-45).
  - Absorption is highly regulated and strongly influenced by luminal chemistry and systemic hepcidin (doc-16, doc-17).

- **Heme iron**
  - Found in meat, especially red meat.
  - Absorbed as an intact **heme complex** via the **heme carrier protein 1 (HCP1)** (doc-05).
  - Once inside enterocytes, heme is degraded by **heme oxygenase** to release ferrous iron (Fe²⁺) (doc-05).
  - Heme-derived supplements (e.g., **heme iron polypeptide**) use the same pathway and are “less affected by hepcidin and food” than non‑heme iron (doc-101).

## 1.2 Apical Uptake: DMT1, HCP1, DCYTB

- **DMT1 (divalent metal transporter 1)**
  - Main transporter for non‑heme Fe²⁺ across the **apical membrane** of duodenal enterocytes (doc-04, doc-45).
  - Requires Fe³⁺→Fe²⁺ reduction before transport (doc-45).
  - Pathology:
    - Rare **DMT1 mutations** cause hypochromic microcytic anemia with hepatic iron loading; oral iron is ineffective and transfusions exacerbate iron overload (doc-227).

- **DCYTB (duodenal cytochrome b)**
  - Apical **ferric reductase** converting Fe³⁺ to Fe²⁺ to enable DMT1-mediated uptake (doc-45).

- **HCP1 (heme carrier protein 1)**
  - Mediates **heme iron uptake** at the apical border (doc-05).
  - Heme iron polypeptide is specifically noted as being absorbed via HCP1 (doc-101).

## 1.3 Basolateral Export: Ferroportin and Hephaestin

- **Ferroportin**
  - The **only known cellular iron exporter**, expressed on basolateral membranes of **enterocytes** and on macrophages (doc-06).
  - Exports Fe²⁺ into the portal circulation.

- **Hephaestin**
  - A **ceruloplasmin-homolog ferroxidase** localized basolaterally in enterocytes (doc-46).
  - Oxidizes Fe²⁺ (exported by ferroportin) to Fe³⁺ for binding to transferrin (doc-46).

## 1.4 Dietary Modifiers of Absorption

- **Inhibitors of non‑heme iron absorption**:
  - **Phytates** (in legumes), **tannins** (in tea), and **calcium** (dairy) all inhibit non‑heme iron absorption (doc-03).
  - Proton-pump inhibitors decrease gastric acid, impairing Fe³⁺ reduction and non‑heme iron absorption (doc-53).

- **Formulations that bypass some regulators**:
  - **Sucrosomial iron**: ferric pyrophosphate encased in a phospholipid + sucrester matrix, **bypassing DMT1** (doc-51).
  - **Heme iron polypeptide**: uses HCP1, less affected by hepcidin and food (doc-101).

## 1.5 Drug and Anatomic Factors Impairing Absorption

- **Drugs**:
  - **Proton-pump inhibitors (PPIs)** reduce gastric acidity, impairing reduction of ferric to ferrous iron and non‑heme uptake (doc-53).
  - (Other classic interactions such as chelation by antacids or some antibiotics are not explicitly in corpus → not in corpus.)

- **Anatomic/GI conditions**:
  - **Bariatric surgery (Roux-en-Y gastric bypass)**:
    - Bypasses the duodenum (major iron absorption site) and reduces acid exposure, frequently causing iron deficiency (doc-54).
  - **Celiac disease**:
    - Should be screened for in adults with iron deficiency without obvious blood loss, especially when oral iron fails (doc-56).
  - **Hookworm infection**:
    - Major cause of iron-deficiency anemia in tropical regions due to chronic intestinal blood loss (doc-98).
  - **Helicobacter pylori**:
    - Can cause refractory iron-deficiency anemia, likely through mucosal sequestration and altered hepcidin, even without overt bleeding (doc-55).

# 2. Plasma Iron Transport

## 2.1 Transferrin: Apo vs Holo

- **Transferrin (TF)**
  - Main iron transport protein in plasma; binds **two Fe³⁺ ions** per molecule (doc-07).
  - Exists as:
    - **Apotransferrin (iron-free)** and
    - **Holotransferrin (diferric)**.
  - The **ratio** of apo- to holo-transferrin reflects transferrin saturation (TSAT) (doc-61).
  - TSAT:
    - **TSAT < 16%** suggests iron deficiency (doc-08).
    - **TSAT > 45%** raises concern for iron overload (doc-08).

## 2.2 Transferrin Receptor 1 (TfR1) and Cellular Delivery

- **TfR1**
  - Mediates cellular uptake of transferrin-bound iron.
  - Highly expressed on **rapidly dividing and iron-deficient cells**, including erythroblasts (doc-09).
  - **Diferric transferrin** has the highest affinity for TfR1, prioritizing delivery of iron-loaded transferrin to high-demand cells like erythroblasts (doc-62).

## 2.3 Transferrin Receptor 2 (TfR2) and Iron Sensing

- **TfR2**
  - Expressed on **hepatocytes and erythroblasts**; functions as a systemic iron sensor (doc-57).
  - Upregulates hepcidin in response to iron-loaded transferrin (doc-57).
  - **TfR1/TfR2/HFE complex** on hepatocyte membranes senses holotransferrin and modulates hepcidin transcription (doc-73).

## 2.4 Soluble Transferrin Receptor (sTfR)

- **sTfR**
  - Circulating form of the TfR1 ectodomain.
  - Elevated in **iron deficiency**, but **unaffected by inflammation**, aiding distinction between iron deficiency anemia (IDA) and anemia of chronic disease (ACD) (doc-10).
  - The **sTfR/log ferritin index**:
    - > 2 favors IDA.
    - < 1 favors ACD (doc-88).

## 2.5 Ceruloplasmin and Hephaestin in Iron Oxidation

- **Hephaestin** (enterocytes) (doc-46) and **ceruloplasmin** (not detailed here beyond homology) function as **ferroxidases**, converting Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺ to load onto transferrin (doc-46).

# 3. Storage: Ferritin Biology and Body Iron Distribution

## 3.1 Ferritin Structure and Function

- **Ferritin**
  - Primary iron storage protein, capable of sequestering up to **~4500 iron atoms** per molecule (doc-11).
  - A **24-subunit heteropolymer** composed of heavy (H) and light (L) chains (doc-63).
  - **H-chain**:
    - Encoded by **FTH1**; has **ferroxidase activity** converting Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺ for safe storage (doc-63).
  - **L-chain**:
    - Encoded by **FTL**; stabilizes the mineral core and enhances long-term storage (doc-63).

## 3.2 Ferritin Gene Mutations

- **FTH1 mutations**
  - Cause autosomal-dominant **adult-onset hyperferritinemia** with **iron-deficient erythropoiesis** (doc-64).

- **FTL (L-chain) mutations**
  - Disrupt iron-responsive element (IRE) and cause **hereditary hyperferritinemia–cataract syndrome**:
    - Marked elevation of ferritin.
    - Bilateral early cataracts (doc-65, doc-209).

- **Interpretation of high ferritin**
  - Ferritin >1000 ng/mL with **normal TSAT** suggests hyperferritinemia syndromes (FTL mutations, Still disease, MAS, etc.) or non–iron overload etiologies (doc-207).

## 3.3 Mitochondrial Ferritin

- **Mitochondrial ferritin (FTMT)**
  - Expressed in iron-loaded tissues.
  - Thought to **protect mitochondria from iron-induced oxidative damage** (doc-66).

## 3.4 Hemosiderin

- **Hemosiderin**
  - Insoluble iron storage form derived from **degraded ferritin**, forming aggregated iron-protein complexes (doc-67, doc-13).
  - Accumulates in macrophages and tissues in iron overload states (doc-13).
  - Histologically visible with **Perls Prussian blue** stain (doc-67).

*(Formal quantitative body compartment distribution—e.g., 2–3 g in RBCs, etc.—is not explicitly detailed in corpus; not in corpus.)*

# 4. Cellular Uptake and Iron–Sulfur Cluster Biogenesis

## 4.1 Cellular Iron Uptake

- **Transferrin–TfR1 pathway**
  - Diferric transferrin binds TfR1, gets endocytosed into clathrin-coated vesicles; iron is released in acidified endosomes and transported into cytosol, primarily via **DMT1** (doc-09, doc-04). (Specific vesicular steps are not exhaustively described in corpus → partial, not in corpus for some mechanistic detail.)

- **Ferroportin**
  - Opposition to uptake: exports cellular iron (enterocytes, macrophages), regulated by hepcidin (doc-06, doc-16).

## 4.2 Iron–Sulfur (Fe–S) Cluster Biogenesis

- **Fe–S clusters**
  - Essential cofactors in **mitochondrial electron transport, DNA repair, and ribosome assembly** (doc-200).
- **Mitochondrial export to cytosol**
  - **ABCB7** export is critical: cytosolic Fe–S clusters require ABCB7-mediated export from mitochondria (doc-202).
  - **ABCB7 mutations** cause X-linked sideroblastic anemia with ataxia (doc-202), linking Fe–S cluster biology to sideroblastic phenotypes (see §11).

# 5. The Hepcidin–Ferroportin Axis

## 5.1 Central Role of Hepcidin

- **Hepcidin**
  - Liver-derived peptide hormone and **master regulator of iron homeostasis** (doc-15).
  - Binds ferroportin, causing its **internalization and degradation**, thereby blocking iron export from enterocytes and macrophages (doc-16).
  - Upregulated by:
    - **Iron repletion** via **BMP6/SMAD** pathway (doc-17, doc-70).
    - **Inflammation** via **IL‑6/STAT3** pathway (doc-17, doc-74).
  - Dysregulation underlies many iron disorders: hemochromatosis (low hepcidin), ACD (high hepcidin), IRIDA (inappropriately high hepcidin) (doc-28, doc-34, doc-156).

## 5.2 BMP6/SMAD Pathway (Iron-Sensing)

- **BMP6 (bone morphogenetic protein 6)**
  - Principal ligand for hepcidin transcription activation via **SMAD1/5/8** pathway (doc-70).
- **Mechanism**
  - BMP6 binds to BMP receptors with co-receptor **hemojuvelin (HJV)** (HJV not deeply detailed here beyond being a co-receptor; its precise text is not in the retrieved abstracts → not in corpus for mechanistic exposition).
  - Signals via phosphorylated SMAD1/5/8 complexes to drive **HAMP** (hepcidin) transcription (doc-70).

## 5.3 IL‑6/STAT3 Pathway (Inflammation)

- **IL‑6**
  - Inflammation-induced cytokine that activates **STAT3**.
- **IL‑6/STAT3**:
  - IL‑6–driven hepcidin increase is mediated by STAT3 binding to a **proximal STAT3 element** in the HAMP promoter (doc-74).
  - Drives ACD by increasing hepcidin and trapping iron in macrophages (doc-34, doc-146).

## 5.4 Erythroferrone and Erythroid Regulators

- **Erythroferrone (ERFE)**
  - Secreted by erythroblasts in response to **erythropoietin (EPO)** stimulation (doc-76).
  - Sequesters BMP ligands, suppressing hepcidin and mobilizing iron to sustain erythropoiesis (doc-76).
  - Overproduction in **thalassemia** contributes to hepcidin suppression and iron overload (doc-168, doc-167).

- **Twisted gastrulation (TWSG1)**
  - BMP modulator co-secreted with ERFE in thalassemic ineffective erythropoiesis (doc-79).

## 5.5 HFE, TfR2, TMPRSS6, and Other Modulators

- **HFE**
  - Mutations (e.g., C282Y) in HFE cause classical type 1 hemochromatosis via impairment of hepcidin sensing of body iron stores, resulting in inappropriately low hepcidin and excessive intestinal absorption (doc-28, doc-118).
  - HFE forms a complex with **TfR1 and TfR2** on hepatocytes, involved in sensing diferric transferrin and regulating hepcidin (doc-73).

- **TfR2**
  - Hepatic and erythroid iron sensor that **upregulates hepcidin** when iron-loaded transferrin is abundant (doc-57).

- **TMPRSS6 (matriptase‑2)**
  - Negative regulator of hepcidin. Loss-of-function mutations lead to **inappropriately high hepcidin despite iron deficiency**, i.e. IRIDA (doc-156, doc-37).
  - Target of **TMPRSS6-ASO / siRNA investigational therapies** to purposely raise hepcidin in iron-loading anemias (doc-234).

# 6. Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA)

## 6.1 Epidemiology and Public Health Burden

- IDA is the **most common nutritional deficiency worldwide**, affecting ~1.2 billion people (doc-21).
- WHO estimates:
  - 30% of women and 40% of preschool children worldwide have anemia, **most attributable to iron deficiency** (doc-235).
- Major public health issue; see §18.

## 6.2 Pathophysiology

- Absolute iron deficit leads to:
  - Reduced hemoglobin synthesis → microcytic hypochromic anemia.
  - Depleted ferritin and TSAT < 16% (doc-08, doc-12).
- Etiologies often cumulative: blood loss, poor intake, malabsorption, increased requirements (doc-21, doc-54, doc-92).

## 6.3 Etiologies

- **Menstrual blood loss**
  - Leading cause of IDA in pre-menopausal women; **menorrhagia warrants gynecologic evaluation** (doc-92).

- **Gastrointestinal blood loss**
  - **Colorectal cancer** must be ruled out in iron-deficient adult men and post-menopausal women without an obvious source (doc-93).
  - **Hookworm infection** as a major cause in endemic areas (doc-98).

- **Malabsorption and anatomic factors**
  - **Celiac disease** screening recommended in otherwise unexplained adult IDA, particularly if oral iron fails (doc-56).
  - **Bariatric (Roux-en-Y) surgery**: common cause due to excluded duodenum and reduced acid (doc-54).
  - **H. pylori**: can cause refractory IDA independent of overt bleeding (doc-55).

- **Dietary insufficiency**
  - Especially in children, pregnant women, and populations with limited meat intake (global data: doc-21, doc-235).

- **IRIDA and rare genetic causes**
  - IRIDA: TMPRSS6 mutations causing poor oral iron response (see §10) (doc-37, doc-38).

## 6.4 Diagnostic Patterns

- **Key lab markers**:
  - Low hemoglobin and hematocrit with **low MCV/MCH**.
  - **Serum ferritin**:
    - Reflects body iron stores but is an acute-phase reactant (doc-12).
  - **TSAT < 16%** suggests iron deficiency (doc-08).
  - Low serum iron, high TIBC (not explicitly detailed in corpus but consistent with standard practice—core TIBC detail itself not in corpus).
  - **sTfR elevated** in IDA, normal in isolated ACD (doc-10).
  - **sTfR/log ferritin index > 2** supports IDA (doc-88).

- **Caveats**:
  - Ferritin can be normal or elevated in concurrent inflammation; ferritin >100 ng/mL (in non-CKD, non-ACD) makes pure IDA unlikely but does not exclude **combined IDA + ACD** (doc-150).
  - Bone marrow iron stain is rarely used now but remains a definitive test (doc-91).

## 6.5 Oral Iron Therapy

### 6.5.1 Conventional Salts and Elemental Iron Content

- **Standard preparations** (per 325 mg tablet) (doc-100):

  | Preparation           | Elemental iron per 325 mg | Notes                                 |
  |-----------------------|---------------------------|----------------------------------------|
  | Ferrous sulfate       | 65 mg                     | Most commonly used (doc-100).         |
  | Ferrous gluconate     | 36 mg                     | Better tolerated, less iron (doc-100). |
  | Ferrous fumarate      | 106 mg                    | Highest elemental iron (doc-100).     |

- **Liquid formulations**:
  - Ferrous sulfate elixir is useful in pediatrics and in patients with dysphagia; may stain teeth and should be taken via straw (doc-102).

- **Slow-release formulations**
  - Often **bypass duodenal absorptive sites** and have inferior absorption compared to immediate-release ferrous sulfate (doc-103).

- **Alternative oral forms**
  - **Sucrosomial iron**: DMT1-independent uptake; better tolerated in some settings (doc-51).
  - **Iron polysaccharide complexes / ferric maltol**: good GI tolerance with similar efficacy to ferrous sulfate in IBD (doc-50).
  - **Heme iron polypeptide**: absorbed via HCP1, less impacted by hepcidin and food (doc-101).

### 6.5.2 Dosing, Response, and Monitoring

- **Monitoring**:
  - Hemoglobin should be checked **2–4 weeks** after starting oral iron; adequate responders show a clear Hb rise (doc-210).
  - Failure to respond prompts assessment of adherence, GI blood loss, and consideration of IRIDA (doc-210).

## 6.6 Intravenous (IV) Iron Formulations

### 6.6.1 Indications

- IV iron indicated for:
  - Malabsorption or prior bariatric surgery.
  - Intolerance or failure of oral iron.
  - Chronic blood loss requiring rapid repletion.
  - CKD on ESAs, particularly hemodialysis patients (doc-26, doc-117, doc-151).

### 6.6.2 Common IV Preparations and Dosing

- **Iron sucrose (Venofer)**
  - Typical dose: **100–200 mg** per session; multiple sessions needed to replete stores (doc-109).

- **Iron dextran (INFeD, DexFerrum)**
  - Allows **total-dose infusion**, but has the **highest rate of severe hypersensitivity** (doc-110).

- **Ferric carboxymaltose (Injectafer)**
  - Can give **750 mg per infusion**, up to 1500 mg total over weeks, with low anaphylaxis rate (doc-111).

- **Ferric derisomaltose (Monoferric)**
  - Single infusion of **1000–1500 mg** with infusion reaction rates similar to ferric carboxymaltose (doc-113).

- **Ferumoxytol (Feraheme)**
  - Carbohydrate-coated iron oxide, initially an MRI contrast agent; now used for iron repletion (doc-112).

### 6.6.3 Adverse Effects and Monitoring

- **Hypersensitivity reactions**
  - Highest with iron dextran; lower with newer preparations (doc-110, doc-111, doc-113).

- **Hypophosphatemia**
  - Class effect of IV iron, **most pronounced with ferric carboxymaltose**, linked to FGF23 activation (doc-114).

- **Monitoring**
  - Hemoglobin at 2–4 weeks (as with oral) for response (doc-210).
  - Ferritin and TSAT rechecked **~3 months** after a course; risk of hepatic iron deposition with over-replacement (doc-211).

# 7. Hereditary Hemochromatosis (HH)

## 7.1 Types and Genetic Basis

- **Type 1 (HFE-related)**
  - Most common; due to **HFE mutations** (C282Y homozygosity or C282Y/H63D compound heterozygosity) (doc-118, doc-27, doc-131).
  - Leads to **inappropriately low hepcidin**, excessive absorption, and parenchymal iron deposition (doc-28).

- **Type 2A (juvenile, HJV) and 2B (HAMP)**
  - **Type 2B**: HAMP (hepcidin) gene mutations causing severe juvenile hemochromatosis (doc-120).
  - Type 2A (HJV-related) not detailed in retrieved documents → not in corpus.

- **Type 3 (TfR2)**
  - **TfR2 mutations**; phenotype intermediate between type 1 and juvenile forms (doc-121).

- **Type 4 (ferroportin disease)**
  - **Type 4A (loss-of-function)**:
    - Macrophage iron loading, normal-to-high ferritin but **low TSAT** (doc-122).
  - **Type 4B (gain-of-function)**:
    - Hepatocyte iron loading, phenotype resembling HFE hemochromatosis (doc-123).

## 7.2 Pathophysiology

- **HFE-hemochromatosis**
  - Impaired hepatic sensing of iron → **low hepcidin** → increased ferroportin activity and intestinal absorption (doc-28).
- **Juvenile forms (HJV/HAMP)**
  - Near-complete absence of hepcidin → early, severe iron loading (doc-120).
- **Ferroportin disease**
  - Loss-of-function: reduced iron export, macrophage iron sequestration (doc-122).
  - Gain-of-function: reduced hepcidin sensitivity, excessive export and hepatocyte loading (doc-123).

## 7.3 Diagnosis and Screening

- **Initial screening**
  - Serum ferritin and TSAT; **TSAT > 45%** is threshold to proceed with HFE genotyping (doc-130, doc-30).
- **Diagnostic criteria**
  - Elevated TSAT and ferritin plus compatible HFE genotype (doc-30, doc-118).
- **HFE mutation panel**
  - Typically includes C282Y, H63D, S65C variants (doc-131).
- **Hepcidin assays**
  - Not yet routine, but may help distinguish HH subtypes and monitor chelation (doc-133).
- **Family screening**
  - Not spelled out in corpus wording, but genetic testing of first-degree relatives is implicitly supported by the genetic nature of HH (general principle; explicit structured guideline not in corpus).

## 7.4 Treatment: Phlebotomy and Chelation

- **Phlebotomy**
  - First-line treatment; each unit removes ~**200–250 mg iron** (doc-31).
  - Used to achieve and maintain near-normal ferritin and TSAT (explicit thresholds not detailed in corpus).

- **Chelation**
  - Used when phlebotomy is contraindicated (e.g., anemia, cardiac disease) and in transfusional iron overload (doc-33).
  - See §8 for details on specific chelators.

- **Erythrocytapheresis**
  - Can remove **2–3× more iron per session** than standard phlebotomy; preferred in some severe overload cases (doc-135).

# 8. Iron Chelators

## 8.1 Overview and Indications

- Used in:
  - **Transfusional iron overload**, especially in thalassemia major (doc-166, doc-33).
  - Situations where phlebotomy is contraindicated (anemia, cardiac compromise) (doc-33).
  - **Acute iron toxicity** (deferoxamine) (doc-214).

## 8.2 Agents, Stoichiometry, and Dosing

- **Deferoxamine (DFO)**
  - **Hexadentate** chelator; binds iron **1:1** (doc-138).
  - Administered subcutaneously over **8–12 hours nightly via pump** (doc-138).
  - **Drug of choice for acute iron toxicity**; gastric decontamination is largely abandoned (doc-214).

- **Deferasirox (DFX)**
  - Oral **tridentate** chelator; binds iron in a **2:1** ratio (two DFX:one Fe³⁺) (doc-139).
  - Once-daily dosing (doc-139).
  - Adverse effects:
    - Renal tubular dysfunction, Fanconi-like syndrome, hepatic injury, rarely fatal multi-organ failure (doc-142).

- **Deferiprone (DFP)**
  - Oral **bidentate** chelator; binds iron **3:1** (three DFP:one Fe³⁺) (doc-140).
  - Taken three times daily (doc-140).
  - Noted for **unique efficacy in cardiac iron chelation** (doc-140).

## 8.3 Combination Therapy

- **DFO + DFP**
  - Used in severe cardiac iron overload when monotherapy fails (doc-141).

- **Monitoring and Toxicities**
  - DFO: risk of ototoxicity and retinopathy at high doses or low iron states; requires annual audiology and ophthalmology (doc-144).
  - DFX: renal and hepatic injury, rare fatal cases (doc-142).
  - DFP: agranulocytosis risk (well known clinically but not explicitly in corpus; not in corpus).

# 9. Anemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)

## 9.1 Pathophysiology

- ACD (anemia of inflammation) results from:
  - **Elevated hepcidin** in chronic inflammatory states, sequestering iron in macrophages and reducing intestinal absorption (doc-34, doc-17).
  - Cytokine-mediated mechanisms:
    - **IL‑6, IL‑1, TNF‑α, IFN‑γ** collectively elevate hepcidin, blunt EPO production, and shorten RBC survival (doc-146).
- Anemia of CKD is a specific form of ACD with **decreased EPO production** plus iron-restricted erythropoiesis (doc-151).

## 9.2 Clinical and Laboratory Features

- Typically **mild-to-moderate normocytic normochromic anemia**, Hb 8–10 g/dL (doc-147).
- Lab pattern:
  - Elevated ferritin (inflammatory acute-phase protein).
  - Low serum iron, low TIBC, low/normal TSAT (doc-35).
- Distinguishing features:
  - Ferritin >100 ng/mL excludes **pure** IDA, but **combined IDA + ACD** remains common (20–30%) (doc-150).
  - sTfR and sTfR/log ferritin index helpful (doc-10, doc-88).

## 9.3 Management

- **Treat underlying inflammation**: cornerstone; iron alone is “largely ineffective” when hepcidin is high (doc-36).
- **Iron therapy**:
  - In CKD, **IV iron** preferred in many scenarios (ESAs, hemodialysis) (doc-117, doc-151).
- **ESAs (epoetin alfa, darbepoetin)**
  - Require adequate iron stores; functional iron deficiency must be corrected (doc-152).
  - CKD anemia: use ESAs and iron repletion to target Hb ~10–11 g/dL (doc-151).

## 9.4 HIF–Prolyl Hydroxylase Inhibitors (HIF‑PHIs)

- Agents: **roxadustat, vadadustat, daprodustat**.
- Mechanism:
  - Stabilize **HIF‑2**, increasing endogenous EPO and improving iron utilization in CKD anemia (doc-154).

## 9.5 Novel Anti-Hepcidin and Pro-Hepcidin Agents

- **Anti-hepcidin agents**
  - Examples: **LJPC‑401, PRS‑080** in development for ACD and beta-thalassemia (doc-153).
- **Mini-hepcidins**
  - Short synthetic peptides mimicking hepcidin, in trials for HH and iron-loading anemias (doc-231).
- **Ferroportin inhibitors**
  - **Vamifeport**: oral inhibitor mimicking hepcidin’s action, in trials for thalassemia and polycythemia vera (doc-233).
- **TMPRSS6-ASO/siRNA**
  - Raise hepcidin by suppressing matriptase‑2 to treat iron-loading anemias (doc-234).

# 10. IRIDA (Iron-Refractory Iron Deficiency Anemia) and TMPRSS6

## 10.1 Genetics and Pathophysiology

- IRIDA:
  - **Autosomal recessive** disorder due to **TMPRSS6 (matriptase‑2) mutations** (doc-37).
- Pathophysiology:
  - Loss of matriptase‑2 function → **inappropriately high hepcidin** despite iron deficiency (doc-156).
  - Leads to poor intestinal iron absorption and iron trapping in stores (doc-38).

## 10.2 Clinical and Laboratory Features

- Labs:
  - Low MCV and MCH.
  - Low TSAT.
  - **Normal-to-high ferritin** despite iron-deficient erythropoiesis (doc-157).
  - Elevated hepcidin (if measured) (doc-157).
- Prevalence:
  - Unknown; likely underdiagnosed because oral iron non-response often attributed to noncompliance (doc-155).
- Heterozygous carriers:
  - May contribute to suboptimal response to oral iron in otherwise unexplained IDA (doc-158).

## 10.3 Diagnosis and Distinction from IDA/ACD

- Suspect IRIDA with:
  - Lifelong microcytosis.
  - Marked **failure of oral iron** despite compliance.
  - Laboratory pattern as above (doc-157, doc-210).
- **Hepcidin response to iron challenge**:
  - Measuring hepcidin after oral iron dose can distinguish hepcidin-driven malabsorption (IRIDA) from absolute deficiency (doc-212).

## 10.4 Treatment

- Oral iron largely ineffective.
- **IV iron is the mainstay** of therapy (doc-38).
- Some patients achieve adequate Hb but **never normalize MCV or fully replete stores** (doc-159).

# 11. Sideroblastic Anemias

## 11.1 Overview

- Characterized by **ring sideroblasts** in marrow: iron-loaded mitochondria surrounding the nucleus.
- Pathogenesis strongly linked to **mitochondrial heme synthesis** and Fe–S cluster pathways (doc-202).

## 11.2 X-Linked Sideroblastic Anemia

- **ALAS2 mutations**
  - Cause X-linked sideroblastic anemia.
  - Often responds to **pyridoxine (vitamin B6)** supplementation (doc-162).

## 11.3 Acquired Clonal Sideroblastic Anemia

- Associated with **myelodysplastic syndromes with ring sideroblasts (MDS-RS)**.
- Frequently harbors **SF3B1** splicing mutations (doc-163).

## 11.4 Drug-Induced Sideroblastic Anemia

- Caused by:
  - **Isoniazid**
  - **Chloramphenicol**
  - **Linezolid**
  - **Lead poisoning** (doc-164).

## 11.5 Fe–S Cluster Export Defects

- **ABCB7 mutations**
  - Cause X-linked sideroblastic anemia with ataxia, via impaired mitochondrial Fe–S cluster export (doc-202).

# 12. Thalassemia, Ineffective Erythropoiesis, and Luspatercept

## 12.1 Thalassemia and Iron Overload

- **β-thalassemia major**
  - Patients become transfusion-dependent in early childhood; chronic transfusions cause iron overload, the **leading cause of death** (cardiac, hepatic complications) (doc-166).
- **Non-transfusion-dependent thalassemia (NTDT)**
  - Develops iron overload **primarily from increased absorption** due to hepcidin suppression (doc-167).

## 12.2 Ineffective Erythropoiesis and Erythroferrone

- Ineffective erythropoiesis drives:
  - High **erythroferrone** secretion, which sequesters BMPs, suppressing hepcidin and exacerbating iron loading (doc-168, doc-76, doc-167).
  - TWSG1 co-secretion modulates BMP signaling (doc-79).

## 12.3 Luspatercept and Erythroferrone-Targeted Therapies

- **Luspatercept**
  - Activin receptor ligand trap that diminishes late-stage ineffective erythropoiesis.
  - Reduces erythroferrone and improves anemia in **β-thalassemia** and **MDS-RS** (doc-169).

- **Erythroferrone-neutralizing antibodies**
  - Experimental strategy to restore hepcidin and reduce iron overload in β-thalassemia (doc-232).

# 13. Special Populations

## 13.1 Pregnancy

- Iron requirements increase from **18 mg/day to 27 mg/day** during pregnancy due to fetal needs and plasma volume expansion (doc-39).
- IDA in pregnancy is common and clinically important (epidemiology: doc-21, doc-235).

## 13.2 Pediatric Populations

- Iron deficiency anemia is particularly prevalent in preschool children (doc-235).
- **Exclusively breastfed infants beyond 6 months** are at risk; AAP recommends iron-fortified cereals or supplementation (doc-178).
- Liquid iron (ferrous sulfate elixir) is useful; risk of tooth staining (doc-102).

## 13.3 Athletes

- Direct athlete-specific mechanics are not detailed in the retrieved corpus; general concepts of iron loss via sweat/hemolysis are widely known but not explicitly documented here → not in corpus.

## 13.4 Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

- CKD anemia:
  - Mix of ACD and EPO deficiency (doc-151).
  - **Functional iron deficiency**: ferritin >100 ng/mL but TSAT <20% is typical (doc-87, doc-86).
  - KDIGO targets: ferritin >100 ng/mL (HD ≥200 ng/mL) and TSAT >20% (doc-187).
  - IV iron often preferred (doc-117).

## 13.5 Geriatric Patients

- Elderly often have overlapping etiologies: occult GI blood loss (including malignancy), ACD, CKD.
- Explicit geriatric-specific data is limited in corpus beyond general epidemiology (doc-21, doc-93).

# 14. Iron, Immunity, Infection, Cancer, Neurodegeneration

## 14.1 Nutritional Immunity and Host Defense

- **Withholding iron from pathogens** is a key host defense mechanism—“nutritional immunity”:
  - Mediated by **hepcidin**, **lactoferrin**, and **lipocalin-2** (doc-190).
  - Hepcidin sequesters iron in macrophages and reduces serum iron (doc-16, doc-34).
  - Lactoferrin in breast milk and neutrophil granules binds iron and exerts antimicrobial effects (doc-59).

## 14.2 Iron and Infection

- Inflammation-induced hepcidin elevation leads to ACD as part of host defense (doc-34, doc-17, doc-146).
- Specific pathogen-iron interactions (e.g., malaria, TB) are not deeply elaborated in retrieved documents → not in corpus.

## 14.3 Iron and Cancer

- Ferritin and hyperferritinemia syndromes associated with malignancy and inflammatory conditions (doc-207, doc-12).
- Direct mechanistic links between iron metabolism and cancer growth beyond these markers are not robustly detailed in corpus → not in corpus.

## 14.4 Iron and Neurodegeneration

- The corpus does not provide specific mechanistic or clinical data on iron in neurodegenerative disease beyond general oxidative stress concepts → not in corpus.

# 15. Imaging of Iron Overload

## 15.1 MRI T2*and R2* Techniques

- **MRI T2\*** relaxometry:
  - Gold standard for **non-invasive quantification of liver and cardiac iron** (doc-42).
- **Cardiac T2\***:
  - Values:
    - >20 ms: normal.
    - 10–20 ms: mild-moderate overload.
    - <10 ms: severe overload, high risk of cardiomyopathy (doc-69, doc-228).

- **Hepatic R2\***
  - Correlates with **liver iron concentration (LIC)** and is standard for monitoring chelation response (doc-229).

## 15.2 FerriScan (R2 MRI)

- **FerriScan**
  - FDA-cleared quantitative MRI R2 method for LIC measurement (doc-230).
- **Thresholds**:
  - LIC >4 mg/g dry weight indicates overload.
  - LIC >15 mg/g associated with cirrhosis risk (doc-68).

# 16. Laboratory Nuances, Diagnostic Algorithms, Treatment Monitoring

## 16.1 Core Markers

- **Serum ferritin**
  - Reflects body iron stores; also an **acute-phase reactant** increased in inflammation, infection, malignancy (doc-12).
- **Serum iron and TSAT**
  - TSAT <16% suggests iron deficiency; >45% suggests overload (doc-08).
  - Fasting morning samples recommended due to diurnal variation (doc-217).

- **sTfR and sTfR/log ferritin index**
  - Elevated sTfR reflects iron-deficient erythropoiesis (doc-10).
  - sTfR/log ferritin index >2 favors IDA, <1 favors ACD (doc-88).

- **Hepcidin**
  - Assays not routine, but useful in research and complex cases (HH subtypes, chelation monitoring) (doc-133).

## 16.2 Diagnostic Algorithms (Conceptual)

- **For microcytic anemia**:
  - Step wise:
    - Ferritin, TSAT → low ferritin/TSAT favors IDA; high ferritin with low TSAT suggests ACD or IRIDA (doc-12, doc-34, doc-157).
    - sTfR, sTfR/log ferritin ratio when mixed ACD + IDA suspected (doc-88).
    - Consider HFE testing where TSAT >45% and ferritin elevated (doc-30, doc-130).
    - Evaluate for celiac, GI blood loss, H. pylori, heavy menstruation (doc-56, doc-93, doc-55, doc-92).

- **Monitoring response to therapy**:
  - Hb at 2–4 weeks post-therapy initiation (oral or IV) (doc-210).
  - Ferritin & TSAT ~3 months post-IV course to avoid over-treatment (doc-211).

# 17. Research Frontiers

## 17.1 Hepcidin Mimetics and Modulators

- **Mini-hepcidins**
  - Synthetic peptides mimicking hepcidin; in development for HH and iron-loading anemias (doc-231).

- **Ferroportin inhibitor: Vamifeport**
  - Oral agent that blocks ferroportin, mimicking hepcidin’s action.
  - Being tested in **thalassemia and polycythemia vera** (doc-233).

- **TMPRSS6-ASO and siRNA**
  - Suppress matriptase‑2 to **increase hepcidin**, for treatment of iron-loading anemias (doc-234).

## 17.2 Anti-Hepcidin and Erythroferrone-Targeted Agents

- **Anti-hepcidin drugs**
  - LJPC‑401, PRS‑080 under evaluation for ACD and β-thalassemia (doc-153).

- **Erythroferrone-neutralizing antibodies**
  - Aim to restore hepcidin levels in β-thalassemia, reducing iron overload (doc-232).

## 17.3 HIF-PHIs and Beyond

- **HIF-PHIs** (roxadustat, vadadustat, daprodustat)
  - Already in advanced development or use in CKD anemia, enhancing endogenous EPO and iron mobilization (doc-154).

# 18. Public Health and Operative Considerations

## 18.1 Global Burden and Fortification

- IDA: ~1.2 billion affected globally (doc-21); high prevalence in women and young children (doc-235).
- **Fortification programs**:
  - Iron fortification of wheat flour and infant formula has substantially reduced national IDA rates in many countries (doc-223).
  - Over 80 countries have adopted iron fortification; **salt iron fortification** is an emerging large-scale strategy (doc-236).

## 18.2 Screening and Prevention

- **Infants/children**:
  - AAP recommends iron-fortified cereals or supplementation after 6 months for exclusively breastfed infants (doc-178).

- **Women and pregnancy**:
  - Routine supplementation in pregnancy due to increased requirements (doc-39, doc-235).

- **Adults**:
  - Evaluation of iron deficiency includes ruling out serious sources like colorectal cancer (doc-93).

## 18.3 Perioperative Iron Optimization and Patient Blood Management

- **Patient blood management (PBM) programs**:
  - Combine **preoperative iron repletion**, intraoperative cell salvage, and **restrictive transfusion thresholds** (doc-239).
  - IV iron used for rapid correction when surgery is imminent (<4 weeks) (doc-117, doc-26).

# Take-Home Points

1. **Iron absorption is tightly regulated**, with DMT1 and HCP1 mediating non‑heme and heme uptake respectively, DCYTB reducing ferric iron, and ferroportin–hephaestin coupling export to transferrin (doc-04, doc-05, doc-45, doc-46).

2. **Hepcidin is the master regulator**: it down-regulates ferroportin and is controlled by BMP6/SMAD (iron load), IL‑6/STAT3 (inflammation), and erythroferrone (erythropoiesis) (doc-15, doc-16, doc-17, doc-70, doc-74, doc-76).

3. **Transferrin and ferritin** are central: transferrin transports Fe³⁺ (TSAT <16% suggests deficiency, >45% overload), while ferritin stores iron but is also an acute-phase reactant (doc-07, doc-08, doc-11, doc-12, doc-61).

4. **Iron deficiency anemia** is the leading nutritional deficiency worldwide, predominantly affecting women and children; causes include menstrual and GI blood loss, malabsorption (celiac, bariatric), infections (hookworm, H. pylori), and genetic IRIDA (doc-21, doc-92, doc-54, doc-56, doc-55, doc-98, doc-37).

5. **Oral iron therapy** relies on correct formulation and dosing; ferrous sulfate remains standard, but newer formulations (sucrosomial iron, ferric maltol, heme iron polypeptide) may help in specific contexts (doc-100, doc-50, doc-51, doc-101, doc-103).

6. **IV iron** is crucial when absorption or tolerance is limited or in CKD and pre-operative settings; ferric carboxymaltose, iron sucrose, iron dextran, ferric derisomaltose, and ferumoxytol all have distinct dosing and safety profiles (doc-26, doc-109, doc-110, doc-111, doc-113, doc-112, doc-114).

7. **Hereditary hemochromatosis** and related disorders derive from dysregulation of hepcidin or ferroportin (HFE, HJV/HAMP, TfR2, SLC40A1), leading to organ iron overload; phlebotomy is first-line, with chelators and erythrocytapheresis used when needed (doc-28, doc-118, doc-120, doc-121, doc-122, doc-123, doc-31, doc-33, doc-135).

8. **Iron chelators** (deferoxamine, deferasirox, deferiprone) differ in binding stoichiometry, route, and toxicity; combination therapy is often used for severe cardiac iron overload (doc-138, doc-139, doc-140, doc-141, doc-142, doc-144).

9. **Anemia of chronic disease** is driven by cytokine-induced hepcidin and EPO suppression; management focuses on treating inflammation, optimizing iron (often IV), and careful ESA and HIF‑PHI use in CKD (doc-34, doc-35, doc-36, doc-146, doc-151, doc-154).

10. **IRIDA** illustrates the importance of TMPRSS6 and hepcidin in iron homeostasis: high hepcidin, low TSAT, normal/high ferritin, and poor oral iron response; IV iron is the main therapy (doc-37, doc-38, doc-156, doc-157, doc-159).

11. **Sideroblastic anemias** and **thalassemia** highlight mitochondrial iron handling and erythroid regulators: ALAS2, ABCB7, SF3B1, erythroferrone, and Luspatercept-mediated modulation of ineffective erythropoiesis (doc-162, doc-202, doc-163, doc-168, doc-166, doc-169).

12. **Special populations** (pregnant women, children, CKD patients, bariatric surgery recipients) have distinctive iron needs and risks; guidelines emphasize prevention, early detection, and tailored oral/IV strategies (doc-39, doc-178, doc-21, doc-117, doc-151, doc-54).

13. **Iron and immunity**: hepcidin, lactoferrin, and lipocalin-2 mediate nutritional immunity by restricting pathogen access to iron, contributing to ACD (doc-190, doc-59, doc-34).

14. **MRI T2* and R2** have transformed iron overload assessment, especially for liver and heart, and guide chelation intensity (doc-42, doc-69, doc-228, doc-229, doc-230, doc-68).

15. **Public health strategies**—including iron fortification, supplementation in pregnancy and infancy, and perioperative iron optimization—are critical to reducing the enormous global burden of iron deficiency while balancing risks of iron overload (doc-21, doc-223, doc-236, doc-178, doc-239, doc-117).

This corpus underscores a central theme: virtually every major iron disorder—from global IDA to HH, ACD, IRIDA, and thalassemia—is best understood as a disturbance in the tightly coordinated hepcidin–ferroportin–erythropoiesis axis and its interplay with systemic inflammation, genetics, and environmental factors.
